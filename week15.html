🔥 Statement Win

Moltisanti’s Mistress Beats Down Wagner

Moltisanti’s Mistress defeats Wagner 770.13 – 560.73

An absolute beatdown.

Wagner’s troubles continue, while Moltisanti’s Mistress continues the absolute tear he has been on. Wagner’s lineup finally woke up a bit, but it did not matter because Moltisanti’s lineup continues going crazy every single week.

Moltisanti’s Mistress dominated at the plate, outscoring Wagner’s lineup 469–313. He was powered by Otto Lopez with 100, Junior Caminero with 54, Mickey Moniak with 60, and Yordan Alvarez with 76.

The pitching was solid on both sides, but Moltisanti’s Mistress still won that battle too, outscoring Wagner’s pitchers 304–247.

Moltisanti’s Mistress beat Wagner top to bottom.

For Wagner, the concern is real. This is now four straight weeks without cracking 600 points. The record still looks good at 12-3, but the production has fallen off harder than the Yankees in June. Maybe those trades are starting to bite. Maybe the collapse is coming.

Despite Wagner's struggles, anybody facing Moltisanti's Mistress right now is walking into what looks like an unwinnable matchup. With the way this lineup is producing, this team looks terrifying.

📈 Statement Week

VinSalv Strikes Back

VinSalv defeats Team Lill 812.93 – 485.13

VinSalv strikes!

What a fuckin week for this guy. It's amazing what setting your lineup can do. After taking weeks of abuse for forgetting about fantasy baseball, VinSalv finally gave the league something else to talk about. You drop 800 points, and the boo birds quiet down... at least for a little while. He may also see a bump in the softball lineup👀.

VinSalv was led by Eury Pérez, the apple of his eye. Vin never wavered while Pérez sat on the injured list. He held onto him like a Reddit trader with diamond hands refusing to sell GameStop at the peak. The patience paid off in a massive way as Pérez delivered 90.72 points, carrying a perfect game into the seventh inning on Sunday.

He also got a monster week from Dansby Swanson, who apparently remembered how to play baseball, exploding for 78.3 points while launching five DING DONGS.

On the other side, the bad luck continues for Team Lill. He has officially taken JohnnySacks title for unluckiest manager. Fresh off last week's statement win over Justin and finally back above .500, he immediately falls back below it, dropping to 7–8.

Team Lill's lineup managed just 252 points, but the real disaster came from the rotation. Matthew Boyd (-7), Stephen Kolek (1), Cam Schlittler (-30), Paul Skenes (-9), and Michael Wacha (-2) combined to bury any chance of competing.

Just a few weeks ago, Skenes, Schlittler, and Wacha looked like the foundation of one of the league's best rotations. This week they looked more like a demolition crew working against their own team.

The talent is still there, but the timing could not be worse. Team Lill now finds himself below .500 once again and facing an uphill climb as the playoff race continues to heat up.

😬 Nobody Wanted It

Bryce Survives Ice

Bryce defeats Ice 559.93 – 549.27

I warned everyone in last week's preview not to overlook Ice in this matchup. I said "Do not be surprised if this one ends up closer than people expect." and the winning margin was only 10 points.

Coming into the week, it looked like Ice had finally turned the corner. Instead, his team immediately drove straight back into the ditch.

Ice looked like he had multiple double agents working against him. Tyler Phillips (-7), Antonio Senzatela (-3), Roki Sasaki (-32), Jack Perkins (-11), Freddy Peralta (-1), and Mitch Keller (-11) all did their best impression of Team Bryce employees.

As if that wasn't enough, the injury plague piled on. Jeremy Peña missed the entire week, while Corey Seager exited Tuesday's game and never returned. If even a one of those disasters don't happen, Ice probably walks away with a fourth straight victory and finds himself right back in the playoff conversation.

Instead, he is left staring at his roster wondering what the hell just happened.

The funny part? Bryce wasn't exactly trying to win either.

Gage Jump, Bryce's self-proclaimed MLB The Show scouting masterpiece, nearly single-handedly blew the matchup. He finished the week with an astonishing -41.87 points, including a brutal -33.2 on Sunday alone.

He also got -12.07 from Dustin May, whose Cinderella run appears to be over, -33 from Logan Webb, who followed up his dominant June by completely lighting July on fire—in the wrong direction—and -3 from Hunter Greene.

Neither team looked particularly interested in winning this matchup, but someone had to. Bryce escapes with the victory and somehow remains in third place at 9–6. The standings say contender. The eye test says... we'll see.

🥱 Free Space

SlammingMuff Handles Sarrel

SlammingMuff defeats Sarrel 620 – 423.67

Not much to be said about this one.

We all know Sarrel has been handing out free wins like he's Oprah.

Jon claims he actually set his lineup this week, but nobody can be sure. It was Fourth of July weekend, and history tells us Jon tends to zone out once he passes Exit 130 on the Parkway.

Fortunately for him, his rotation did all the heavy lifting, putting up 313 points compared to Sarrel's laughable 67.

Jon even left 76 points on the bench— 58 from Yoshinobu Yamamoto and 18 from Shota Imanaga. The Japanese duo must've been watching hentai together on the bench.

Sarrel somehow managed to outdo him, leaving 158 points sitting on his bench. including Hunter Goodman—the #2 ranked catcher in the league with 523 total points. How Hunter Goodman ended up on Sarrel's bench is beyond me. Most teams would kill for that kind of production from a pitcher, let alone a catcher.

Regardless, both these teams are shit and don't care, so fuck em... let's move on.

🔥 Upset of the Week

JohnnySack Takes Down Justin

JohnnySack defeats Justin 749.30 – 704.43

JohnnySack knocks off Justin in an absolute nailbiter.

Unlike most weeks, Justin spent almost the entire matchup projected to win, so there was no Monday concession speech or early crying out of his bitch ass this time.

The tide finally turned over the weekend, as JohnnySack exploded for 154 points on Saturday and another 178 on Sunday to steal the matchup late.

Justin actually edged JohnnySack offensively 303–286, which somehow makes this loss even worse considering JohnnySack played the entire week without a third baseman and an outfielder. Justin still couldn't capitalize.

The story, once again, was the pitching. JohnnySack's rotation outscored Justin's 463–400, and that's despite several of his own starters trying their best to throw the game away.

JohnnySack somehow survived -36 from Tatsuya Imai (who may be freshening up on his Japanese), -19 from Payton Tolle, -11 from Randy Vásquez, and -7 from Mike Paredes.

Somehow, Justin's "Super Bullpen" folded hard AGAIN. He got -16 from Hunter Brown, -8 from Chris Sale... against the Mets of all teams, and -2 from Trade Acquisition of the Year Framber Valdez. Not exactly the cavalry Justin envisioned.

JohnnySack was led offensively by Rafael Devers, who has officially arrived, putting up 72 points. Since May 1, Devers has mashed 16 home runs, 20 doubles, and owns a ridiculous .937 OPS.

He also rostered three of the top five pitchers of the week: Casey Mize (#1, 95.23), Nolan McLean (#3, 74.8), and Sandy Alcantara (#5, 68.33). That's about as good as it gets.

And perhaps there's a lesson to be learned here. You can spend months trying to rob Wagner at gunpoint in every trade negotiation, collecting shiny new toys and convincing yourself you've built an unbeatable superteam... but fantasy baseball has a funny way of balancing the books.

Justice was served this week. The baseball gods looked down upon the trade block, reviewed the surveillance footage, declined the appeal, and handed down their verdict. Despite Wagner losing, Justin still walks away empty-handed, no closer to first place. Sometimes you don't climb to the top by fleecing everyone in sight. Sometimes karma clocks in, puts on the striped shirt, and reminds you that baseball is still played on the field.
